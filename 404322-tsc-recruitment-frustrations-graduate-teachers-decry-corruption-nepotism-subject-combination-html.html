TSC recruitment frustrations: Graduate teachers decry corruption, nepotism and subject combination

- Many graduate teachers have TSC numbers but are yet to be absorbed for more than a decade

- Some have had to do with being BOM teachers earning as little as KSh 5,000 per month

- This is against the backdrop of the education sector experiencing a deficit of teachers countrywide

Eva Langat graduated from one of the top teachers training colleges in 2016, optimistic that she would soon be absorbed by the Teachers Service Commission (TSC). Five years later, she is still a BOM (Boards of Management) teacher.

BOM teachers are trained educators who are yet to be absorbed by TSC and are employed by board schools to bolster their teaching unit, especially in subjects with staff deficiency.

Some earn from as little as KSh 5,000, a figure that rises to KSh 40,000 depending on the institution's financial abilities.

"I have been trying my luck ever since 2017. TSC employs teachers based on their year of graduation. Marks are awarded based on that, but the funny part is when you find yourself a BOM teacher in a school with a TSC-employed teacher who graduated in 2020," said Eva.

She added that an opportunity to get absorbed once knocked at her door, but it came with strings attached.

"My immediate former boss is a sister-in-law to one of the MPs. There is a day he told her to look for market for TSC employment forms which were retailing at KSh 250,000. She approached me, but I didn't have the money. Someone else paid the money and joined last year January," she continued.

That is the same case with Dorothy Kagure, who has been out for 10 years now without being absorbed. As a BOM teacher, she makes roughly KSh 15,000 per month, making it a poor return on an investment after all the time and fees in college.

"They recently announced job opportunities, but it is difficult to get because half the vacancies are filled by handpicked candidates. See, they said that we should not apply because the 2020 merit list is to be used. Now that is where nepotism will come into play," she lamented.

For Martha Malumbe Makwata, it has been 12 years of trying and failing. With all hopes gone, she decided to venture into the grocery business to fend for her children.

"I have worked as a relief teacher, yet I am still waiting for employment in the dark for the past 12 years. I have kids whose father died, and I look at them, waiting for the peanuts I get as a BoG teacher. I had to become a mama mboga to survive as I wait for employment that I have no idea how many more years it will take. It just wears me up," complained Martha.

All the negative factors remaining constant, some teachers, especially those for secondary school, have also been disadvantaged due to wrong subject combinations.

In other words, choices that are flooded with students are in plenty in the job market too.

Combinations like Physics and Chemistry, Maths and Chemistry or Physics and Maths have fewer students who are mostly absorbed as soon as they graduate.

However, English and Literature, Kiswahili, History and CRE, or Maths and Geography take longer periods to be absorbed.

This is against the backdrop of the education sector, experiencing a deficit of teachers countrywide.
