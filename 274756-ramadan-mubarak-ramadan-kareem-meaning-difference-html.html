History of Ramadan

Ramadan, according to the Islamic lunar calendar, is the ninth month of the Islamic year. Generally, the name ‘Ramadan’ is Arabic for ‘sweltering,’ ‘burning,’ or ‘scorching.’ It is a divine and sacred month for Muslims.

The history of Ramadan dates way back to 610CE. This was when the Holy Book, the Quran, was revealed to Prophet Muhammad. Archangel Gabriel was responsible for this revelation. Thus Ramadan marks a time in the Islam lunar calendar when Muslims fast and get closer to God, eventually improving their relationship with God. It is also a period dedicated to prayer, fasting, and rumination by Muslims.

Ramadan Mubarak meaning in English and wishes

Ramadan is a time when Muslims are expected to be generous and kind to each other. During this month, it is standard to hear the phrase “Ramadan Mubarak.” But what exactly does Ramadan Mubarak mean? For starters, ‘Mubarak’ generally means ‘blessed.’ Thus, the ‘Ramadan Mubarak’ greeting may be translated to ‘blessed Ramadan.’

Therefore, if someone wishes you a Ramadan Mubarak, that person wants you to have a blessed Ramadan. Ramadan Mubarak may also mean ‘Ramadan is blessed’ or ‘happy Ramadan.’ Ramadan follows the fourth pillar of Islam; Fasting. Therefore, it is an important aspect of the Islamic religion. There are five pillars of Islam. The other four are:

  • Shahada is the first pillar. It encompasses the declaration of faith in that there is no deity other than God, and that Muhammad is the messenger of God.
  • Prayer is the second pillar and a Muslim is required to pray five times a day.
  • Zakat, the third pillar deals with charitable giving.
  • Hajj, the fifth pillar can also be termed as a pilgrimage. This pillar necessitates that a Muslim should visit the holy town of Mecca located in Saudi Arabia at least once in a lifetime.

    Ramadan Mubarak and Ramadan Kareem differences

    During Ramadan, the two standard greetings used are Ramadan Mubarak and Ramadan Kareem. But what are the differences, and why is one preferred over the other? Ramadan Mubarak is the commonly used phrase because Ramadan Kareem meaning can be deemed inappropriate since it goes against the Islamic teaching.

    Ramadan is based on fasting, which involves denying yourself of specific things, which goes against the generous meaning of the word Kareem. However, some Muslims believe it's okay to use Ramadan Kareem to signify the blessings Allah gives to followers who have observed the fasting.

    Ramadan rules

    Every Muslim is required to participate in the holy month of Ramadan. However, the following groups of people may be exempted: The elderly, pregnant women, breastfeeding mothers, those people suffering chronic illnesses, children who have not yet gone through puberty, women who are menstruating, and those people who are travelling.

    For the most part, they are supposed to make up for missing Ramadan in the future. Alternatively, they could help feed the poor. While there are numerous rules that the Muslim must adhere to during the fasting month, some of the popular and most recognized ones include:

  • Generally, during the period of Ramadan, Muslims are supposed to avoid eating, smoking, sexual activity, drinking, impure thoughts, jealousy, and immoral behaviour.
  • A Muslim is required to fast from dawn to sunset. No food should be consumed or water drank during this period. The only meals taken are Suhur which is the predawn meal before sunrise, and Iftar, which is the sunset meal
  • The five daily prayers should be offered after breaking the fast, but before eating.
  • After dinner, Muslims are required to head to the mosque and offer the Isha prayer.
  • It is optional to end the day with the Taraweeh prayer.
  • While breaking the fast, the Muslim should first begin the meal by eating an odd number of dates, just like Prophet Muhammad did.
