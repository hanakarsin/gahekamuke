Maraga Succession: Martha Koome Says Married Partners Aren't Entitled to Each Other's Premarital Pro

- Justice Martha Koome said no partner should lay a claim to a property that was developed before marriage

- She explained either party in the marriage acquires occupational rights only in the event that they stay together

- Koome observed that the law corresponds with patriarchal dominance since the laws are made in male-dominated parliaments

The Court of Appeal judge Martha Koome has maintained marriage does not entitle one to premarital property.

Appearing before the Judicial Service Commission vetting panel for the chief justice position, Koome said no partner should lay a claim to a property that was developed before marriage.

Koome explained in the event that the spouses stay together and develop the premarital property in question, then either party in the marriage acquires occupational rights.

"Parties to marriage and equality of property rights depend. If I got married today, I have my property; my spouse would not be entitled to my property, the one I had before marriage. If he is a man and got married when he already had the property, that is his property. But when the lady comes in and they developed it further, she, of course, acquires occupational rights. Once you live in it and create other rights within, those rights can be claimed," she responded.

The lady justice was responding to justice Mohamed Warsame, who sought to know her views on marital property rights during the vetting exercise, which entered its third day on Wednesday, April 14.

Feminism

On feminist legal theories, Koome observed that the law corresponds with patriarchal dominance since the laws are made in male-dominated parliaments.

"Laws are made in parliament and the dormant majority in parliament is male. Men are socialised to look at women as second class citizen, people who are supposed to be at home and not in leadership positions,” she noted.
