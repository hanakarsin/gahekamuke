We've heard of Ocampo 6, reveal Waki 20 now, Odinga

- After setting free of six suspects at the International Criminal Court, there are new calls to have more suspects connected with 2007/8 post election violence revealed 

- Odinga argues that this is the only way that the country can be reconciled

Raila Odinga's elder brother, Oburu, now wants the government to reveal the list of all the 20 leaders named in the so-called Waki list who were first named in connection with the 2007/8 post-election violence.

According to him, if the government is serious about reconciliation since the post-election violence, it is prudent, he says, to have leaders named in this list revealed.

The Waki list was compiled after the Commission Investigating Post-Election Violence (Cipev) in 2008 heard testimonies from various sources regarding the chaos that rocked the country.

The rally had been organised by the opposition to counter the one at Afraha Stadium in Nakuru County that had been called by President Uhuru Kenyatta to give thanks after his deputy William Ruto was set free.

The six leaders who had been accused at the ICC also attended including former head of public service Francis Muthaura, former police commissioner Hussein Ali and radio journalist Joshua arap Sang.

Former Orange Democratic Movement leader Henry Kosgey also attended the rally.

But according to Oburu, the full list of the names that were handed over to Koffi Annan who mediated the peace talks after the post-election should be revealed.

Cipev led by former judge of the appeals court Philip Waki had given the list of the 20 leaders to Annan.

It is from this list that former ICC prosecutor Mourhino Ocampo narrowed down on the six who emerged free eight years later.

Soon after the outcome of a bungled election in December 2007, an unprecedented and spontaneous violence  broke out in Kenya leaving 1,333 dead and approximately 650,000 others displaced from their homes mostly in the Rift Valley region.

Communities that hitherto existed in tranquility rose against one another; people were burnt alive, others were hacked with machetes by the roadsides while homes and churches went up in flames.

A dark cloud of death hang around Kenya as the country ushered in a new year of 2008.

Peace was not to be known until former president Mwai Kibaki and protagonist Raila Odinga signed a peace accord that resulted into a coalition government with the latter becoming the prime minister.

To bring to account the perpetrators of the violence and justice to the victims, Cipev was set up.

A bill that was introduced on the floor of Parliament three times to set up a local tribunal for trying the cases was rejected in February 2009, and the house voted for trials at the ICC.
